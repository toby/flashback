# 2009

## Year thesis

2009 is the year late-2000s gloss becomes practical, measured, and location-aware.

The App Store is no longer a novelty. The iPhone 3GS improves speed and video, while Android 1.5 Cupcake and 1.6 Donut make Google's mobile platform more usable across different devices. Apps now compete less as miracles and more as daily tools.

Kickstarter launches and gives independent design a new public script: pitch, prototype, reward, pledge, update. Foursquare launches at SXSW and turns place into check-in, badge, leaderboard, and map. Facebook introduces the Like button. Twitter's real-time publicness accelerates through trending topics and cultural events. Windows 7 arrives with a more confident, usable Aero desktop after Vista's troubled reception.

The feeling of the year: **glossy optimism learning to justify itself with utility**.

2009 still has rounded buttons, skeuomorphic icons, glass, shadows, and shiny badges. But after the crash, those surfaces feel less carefree. Design now has to prove value: help me find a place, fund a project, like a post, record a video, speed up a phone, organize windows, or make a small product possible.

## How 2009 differs from 2008

2008 broke the system and opened the app market. 2009 starts normalizing life inside it.

| From 2008 | To 2009 |
| --- | --- |
| App Store launches | Apps become routine tools with updates, reviews, categories, and video-capable phones |
| Hope graphics dominate campaign culture | Social buttons, trends, and real-time feeds become everyday political and cultural surfaces |
| Airbnb begins under crisis conditions | Kickstarter launches and formalizes crowdfunding as design narrative and public backing |
| First Android phone ships | Android Cupcake and Donut refine UI, keyboard, camera, search, and market behavior |
| Chrome appears as a new browser | Browser speed and web-app performance become assumed competitive issues |
| Financial crisis hits dramatically | Post-crash restraint, value, and usefulness shape design expectation |
| Vista's glass feels overburdened | Windows 7 makes Aero feel calmer, faster, and more usable |

The key shift: 2009 turns late-decade platform experiments into habits: check in, like, back, update, browse, fund, review, and carry.

## Design climate

### The dominant tension

2009 is pulled between **playful measurement** and **post-crash usefulness**.

1. **Playful measurement** - Foursquare badges, app ratings, follower counts, likes, trending topics, leaderboards, and quantified social participation.
2. **Post-crash usefulness** - lean tools, cheaper launches, crowdfunding, better desktop performance, service reliability, and mobile utility.

The year matters because the fun parts of Web 2.0 become data structures. The badge is cute, but it is also a behavioral measurement. The Like button is friendly, but it is also a distribution mechanism. The Kickstarter reward is charming, but it is also a pre-order economy.

### What is emerging

- **Crowdfunding as design medium**: the pitch page becomes prototype, story, budget, reward ladder, and community contract.
- **Location-based social UX**: check-ins turn cities into games made of places, badges, friends, and maps.
- **Real-time social attention**: trends, likes, retweets, and status updates shape visual hierarchy and news rhythm.
- **Mobile video and speed**: iPhone 3GS makes pocket recording and faster app use feel normal.
- **Android plurality**: multiple phones and OS updates make mobile design less singular than the iPhone story.
- **Refined glass desktop**: Windows 7 keeps Aero but emphasizes taskbar usability, window management, and performance.
- **Recession-era pragmatism**: design credibility increasingly comes from usefulness, clarity, price, and trust.

## Typography

### The typographic mood

2009 typography is rounded, actionable, and metadata-heavy.

The interface is full of short commands: like, check in, pledge, back this project, update, share, install, review, search, trend. Type appears in bubbles, buttons, badges, labels, map pins, app names, project headlines, and notification counters.

The question moves from:

> "How can a glossy app attract attention?"

to:

> "How can a small interface action become a social habit?"

### What changes

- **Verb buttons dominate**: like, check in, pledge, install, share, and update are typographic calls to action.
- **Badges become type-image hybrids**: labels, icons, numbers, and rewards fuse into behavioral symbols.
- **Project pages need editorial hierarchy**: Kickstarter requires title, video, story, rewards, progress, and deadline to read clearly.
- **Mobile lists mature**: settings, messages, maps, stores, and feeds demand legible repeated rows.
- **Desktop UI type calms down**: Windows 7 emphasizes readable taskbar labels, clearer system messages, and less frantic glass.

## Graphic design

2009 graphic design is the age of the small social action.

The Like button is visually modest, but historically huge: a tiny sign that approval can be counted, distributed, and designed into the page. Foursquare's badges make participation collectible. Kickstarter project pages combine editorial storytelling, product photography, progress bars, reward tables, and video thumbnails into a new graphic format for independent making.

Late Web 2.0 style is still visible: rounded corners, shiny icons, gradients, speech bubbles, and friendly mascots. But the strongest graphics are functional systems, not standalone images. The graphic object is increasingly a button tied to a database.

## Product and industrial design

2009 product design is mobile, crowdfunded, and post-netbook.

The iPhone 3GS shows that the smartphone's next design story is not only form but speed, camera capability, and software responsiveness. Palm Pre and webOS introduce card-based multitasking and gesture areas, an elegant mobile interaction alternative. Android devices multiply, making hardware diversity part of the design landscape.

Kickstarter changes product design before manufacturing: renderings, prototypes, videos, reward tiers, and community updates become part of how objects come into being. A product can now be designed in public before it fully exists.

## Architecture and interiors

2009 interiors are improvised, connected, and economical.

The post-crash office values laptops, shared desks, coworking energy, cheap furniture, whiteboards, and fast iteration more than lavish build-outs. Crowdfunding and startups make the apartment, studio, garage, and small workshop feel newly credible as design-production spaces.

Location services also alter the city interior: cafes, bars, airports, galleries, and shops become check-in targets. A venue now has a digital layer of tips, badges, friends, maps, and status.

## Fashion and self-design

2009 self-design is real-time and recession-filtered.

The self is increasingly made of updates, likes, check-ins, profile photos, phone videos, and location traces. Street style blogs, Tumblr, Facebook albums, and Twitter make outfits circulate quickly. Recession culture encourages mixing high and low, thrift, DIY, and visible resourcefulness.

The phone remains an accessory, but it is now also a camera, map, video recorder, social meter, and proof of presence. Style is not only what you wear; it is where you checked in and what you posted.

## Music

2009 music design is streaming-adjacent and feed-driven.

Spotify's early European growth, YouTube's massive role in discovery, iTunes downloads, MySpace's fading but still visible band culture, and Twitter fan behavior all reshape music identity. The album cover is increasingly one tile among many: playlist image, video thumbnail, profile avatar, blog embed, store art.

Designers respond with bold small-scale graphics, instant-recognition photography, lyric videos, performance clips, and identities that survive compression into feeds.

## Film and moving image

2009 moving image is spectacular in theaters and casual in pockets.

*Avatar* demonstrates 3D spectacle, motion capture, world-building, and digital environment design at blockbuster scale. At the same time, iPhone 3GS video recording makes everyday moving image more pocketable. YouTube culture keeps normalizing upload, comment, and share behavior.

Motion design also becomes app behavior: card swipes in webOS, app transitions, map animations, check-in confirmations, video uploads, and progress bars. The everyday moving image is a confirmation loop as much as a cinematic event.

## Color, material, and surface

2009 color is glossy but more restrained.

The palette includes Foursquare blues and greens, Kickstarter green, Facebook blue, Twitter blue, Windows 7 glass blue, iPhone black, Android green, Palm dark gloss, recession grey, and warm project-page neutrals. Materials include glass, soft-touch plastic, aluminum, LCD glare, app-icon shine, progress bars, maps, and photographed prototypes.

The shared surface logic is **friendly proof**. Show me the badge, the pledge total, the like count, the check-in, the rating, the progress bar, the taskbar preview, the video, the map.

## Flashback design recipes

### Recipe 1: Check-in city game

Use for: location apps, travel tools, events, neighborhood guides, venue loyalty.

- Palette: bright blue, lime green, white, map beige, badge gold.
- Type: rounded sans, place names, small timestamps, playful badge labels.
- Layout: map plus list, check-in button, friend stack, badge cabinet, leaderboard.
- Imagery: pins, venues, phones, badges, city grids, mayorship crowns.
- Motion: check-in confirm, badge unlock, map pan, friend pop-in.
- Risk: making it look like mature GPS lifestyle design rather than early location game.
- Add accuracy with: mayorships, badges, and slightly awkward venue data.

### Recipe 2: Crowdfunded prototype page

Use for: product launches, maker projects, cultural funding, independent hardware.

- Palette: Kickstarter green, white, charcoal, warm grey, progress-bar blue.
- Type: clear project title, reward tiers, deadline labels, pledge verbs.
- Layout: video hero, story column, right reward rail, progress bar, backer count.
- Imagery: prototypes, sketches, founder video, workshop shots, reward samples.
- Motion: pledge progress, update post, video play, reward select.
- Risk: over-polishing it like a later venture-backed product page.
- Add accuracy with: rough prototypes, earnest narrative, and visible funding mechanics.

### Recipe 3: Post-crash utility app

Use for: finance tools, productivity apps, mobile utilities, public-service products.

- Palette: black glass, sober blue, grey, white, small green success.
- Type: system sans, action verbs, data labels, list rows, notification counts.
- Layout: tab bar, list view, settings, action button, small chart or map.
- Imagery: icons, meters, receipts, maps, checkmarks, plain product screenshots.
- Motion: quick launch, list scroll, confirm, sync spinner, alert badge.
- Risk: too much 2010s flat dashboard polish.
- Add accuracy with: skeuomorphic gloss and early app-store conventions.

### Recipe 4: Refined Aero desktop

Use for: operating systems, enterprise tools, productivity suites, desktop nostalgia.

- Palette: glass blue, charcoal taskbar, white windows, green progress, amber alert.
- Type: Segoe-like system sans, taskbar labels, preview titles, dialog copy.
- Layout: taskbar previews, snapped windows, translucent frames, desktop gadgets.
- Imagery: window glass, folder icons, progress bars, wallpaper light, thumbnails.
- Motion: aero peek, snap, minimize glow, taskbar preview fade.
- Risk: using Vista chaos or later Windows flat tiles.
- Add accuracy with: Windows 7's calmer taskbar and usability correction.

## Anti-cliches

Do not make 2009 look like:

- Instagram, iPad, or mature Pinterest culture; those arrive later.
- Flat Material Design or iOS 7 minimalism.
- Pure recession austerity with no Web 2.0 gloss or badges.
- 2007 first-iPhone novelty; by 2009 apps are everyday tools.
- Kickstarter as a fully professionalized crowdfunding industry.
- Foursquare as generic maps; it is badges, mayorships, and check-ins.
- Windows 8 tiles or modern desktop flatness.
- Android as a mature unified design language.
- Social media without likes, trends, counters, and public metrics.

For 2009, the era should feel like **friendly glossy systems becoming practical, measurable, and post-crash**.
